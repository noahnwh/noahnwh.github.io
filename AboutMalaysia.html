History

Malaysia's history is a colorful tapestry of influences. The earliest records of the region date back to ancient times, with evidence of human habitation dating back 40,000 years. Influenced by Indian and Chinese traders who brought Hinduism and Buddhism to the region in the first millennium B.C., the region became part of a thriving trade network.

In the 15th century, the Malacca Sultanate became a prominent maritime and commercial power, controlling the Strait of Malacca, an important trade route. The spread of Islam in the region during this period marked a major shift in the cultural and religious landscape of Malaysia.

The arrival of the Portuguese in 1511, followed by the Dutch and the British in the 18th and 19th centuries, brought colonial influence to Malaysia. The British took control of the Malay Peninsula and formed the Federation of Malaya in 1895 and later in 1948. During the Second World War, Japan occupied Malaya but the British regained control after the war.

On August 31, 1957, Malaysia gained independence from Britain and was initially known as the Federation of Malaya. 1963 saw the expansion of Malaya into Singapore, Sabah and Sarawak to form Malaysia. Later, Singapore became independent in 1965.

Population

As of 2023, the population of Malaysia will be approximately 33 million. The country's demographic composition is remarkably diverse, reflecting its history of migration and colonization. The main ethnic groups in Malaysia are:

- Malay (about 69%): The largest ethnic group, predominantly Muslim and recognized as the original inhabitants of Malaysia.

- Chinese (about 23%): the second largest group, mainly living in urban areas and engaged in commercial activities.

- Indians (about 7%): They live mainly in urban areas and are engaged in various trades such as agriculture, commerce and medicine.

- Indigenous people (Orang Asli and others) (about 1%): This group comprises various tribes, especially in East Malaysia, each with its own distinct culture and language.

Language

Malaysia's linguistic landscape is as diverse as its population. The official language is Bahasa Malaysia (Malay), which is used in government, education and formal settings. However, English is also widely spoken and understood, particularly in the areas of business, tourism and higher education.

In addition to Malay and English, several other languages and dialects are spoken throughout the country, reflecting the multicultural nature of the population:

- Chinese dialects: including Mandarin, Cantonese and Hokkien.

- Indian languages: such as Tamil, Telugu, Malayalam and Punjabi.

- Indigenous Languages: Languages spoken by the Orang Asli people of Peninsular Malaysia and by numerous indigenous groups in Sabah and Sarawak.

Cultural Diversity

Malaysia's cultural diversity is one of its most distinctive features. This multiculturalism is celebrated throughout the year through various festivals and events such as Hari Raya Aidilfitri, Chinese New Year, Deepavali and Gawai Dayak.

Malaysia's diverse cultural heritage is also reflected in its architecture, music, dance and cuisine. From Islamic art and architecture in the mosques, to the vibrant street art of Penang and the colonial architecture of Malacca, Malaysia showcases a unique blend of traditional and modern influences.

Economy

Malaysia has a strong economy and is one of the strongest economies in Southeast Asia. Malaysia is an important exporter of natural resources such as palm oil, rubber and petroleum. The country is also a major player in the electronics and semiconductor industries, with a growing focus on technology and innovation.

In recent years, Malaysia had become a popular destination for tourism, financial and medical travel. The Government had implemented various initiatives to attract foreign investment and promote sustainable development, with the goal of achieving high-income country status by 2025.
